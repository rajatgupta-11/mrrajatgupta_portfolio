Following the application of MOST-driven workflow changes, assembly cell throughput increased from 29 to 38 valves per shift.

The improvement was achieved by isolating non-value-added motions and tightening the synchronization between manual tasks and shared overhead resources.

System Infrastructure

Focused on industrial butterfly valves, the assembly line integrates hydraulic presses, overhead cranes, and manual clamping stations.

By breaking down the process at the motion level, I identified that ergonomics and station proximity were the primary drivers of latent cycle time.

Methodology: Why MOST?

While traditional time studies rely on stopwatch data to establish averages, they are inherently limited by operator variance and subjective performance rating. We utilized MOST (Maynard Operation Sequence Technique): a predetermined motion time system to provide a more granular, objective analysis.

  • Standardization: MOST focuses on the movement of objects rather than chronometric time, ensuring consistent results across different operators.
  • Non-Value-Added Analysis: It allows us to decompose complex tasks into fundamental "General Moves" or "Controlled Moves," making motion waste immediately visible in the data.
  • Predictive Power: Unlike stopwatch studies which require an active process, MOST can accurately predict cycle times for optimized workflows before they are implemented on the shop floor.

Optimization Strategy

  • Crane Utilization Eliminated 15% of travel time by re-indexing pallet proximity.
  • Task Aggregation Grouped repetitive fastening to minimize operator tool-switching.
  • Automation Flag Identified manual clamping as the highest ROI target for future hydraulic automation.
